On November 13, 2025, the European Commission issued Commission Regulation (EU) 2025/2269, a targeted correction to existing regulations governing the labeling of recycled plastic materials, the development of recycling technologies, and the transfer of authorizations for food contact materials containing recycled plastic.

## Background and Context

Regulation 2025/2269 amends and corrects provisions in the EU's regulatory framework for recycled plastics in food contact materials. The original regulation — Commission Regulation (EC) No 282/2008 — established the rules for recycled plastic materials and articles intended to come into contact with foods.

As the recycled plastic market has surged in response to the EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR) and its mandatory recycled content targets, the regulatory framework has needed to evolve to keep pace with rapidly developing recycling technologies and market practices.

## Key Provisions of Regulation 2025/2269

### Labeling Corrections

The regulation corrects and clarifies labeling requirements for recycled plastic materials used in food contact applications. The updated labeling rules ensure that:

- **Clear identification** — Recycled plastic content is clearly labeled, enabling consumers and downstream processors to identify the material composition
- **Accurate claims** — Claims about recycled content are verifiable and consistent across the supply chain
- **Traceability** — Labeling supports traceability requirements that are essential for food safety and regulatory compliance

### Recycling Technology Development

Regulation 2025/2269 addresses the rapid evolution of plastic recycling technologies, including:

- **Chemical recycling** — The regulation provides updated guidance on how chemically recycled plastics are classified and labeled, distinguishing them from mechanically recycled materials
- **Novel recycling processes** — As new recycling technologies emerge (including enzymatic recycling, solvent-based purification, and advanced sorting), the regulation provides a framework for evaluating and approving these processes
- **Decontamination standards** — Updated standards for demonstrating that recycled plastics have been adequately decontaminated for food contact use

### Authorization Transfers

The regulation streamlines the process for transferring authorizations for recycled plastic food contact materials between companies. This is particularly important as the recycling industry consolidates and as companies merge, acquire, or transfer recycling operations.

The updated authorization transfer provisions ensure that:
- Food safety standards are maintained during ownership transitions
- Regulatory continuity is preserved when recycling facilities change hands
- The administrative burden of re-authorization is reduced where the underlying technology and processes remain unchanged

## Market Impact

The issuance of Regulation 2025/2269 comes at a critical time for the European recycled plastics market. The PPWR's mandatory recycled content targets are driving unprecedented demand for food-grade recycled plastic:

- **PET recycling** — Food-grade recycled PET (rPET) demand has surged as brands seek to meet recycled content targets for beverage bottles and food packaging
- **Polyolefin recycling** — Recycled polyethylene and polypropylene for food contact applications represent a growing market segment
- **Chemical recycling** — Advanced recycling technologies that can produce food-grade recycled plastic from mixed waste streams are attracting significant investment

The regulatory clarity provided by Regulation 2025/2269 is expected to facilitate investment in recycling infrastructure by reducing regulatory uncertainty and providing clear rules for market participants.

## Implications for Bioplastics

While Regulation 2025/2269 focuses on recycled conventional plastics, it has important implications for the bioplastics industry:

- **Compostable packaging** — As recycled content requirements tighten, compostable bioplastics offer an alternative end-of-life pathway for food-contaminated packaging that is difficult to recycle
- **Bio-based recycled content** — The regulation's framework for calculating and verifying recycled content may need to accommodate bio-based materials in the future
- **Labeling clarity** — The emphasis on accurate labeling benefits bioplastics producers whose products carry recognized certifications (EN 13432, OK Compost) that provide clear identification

## Industry Response

Industry associations including European Bioplastics, Plastics Recyclers Europe, and the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) have welcomed the regulatory clarity provided by Regulation 2025/2269. The regulation addresses several practical issues that had created uncertainty for recycling operators and food packaging manufacturers.

"The correction provides much-needed clarity on labeling requirements and authorization transfers," said industry representatives. "This will help facilitate the investment needed to meet the EU's ambitious recycled content targets."

## Looking Forward

Regulation 2025/2269 is part of an ongoing process of regulatory refinement as the EU's circular economy framework continues to evolve. Further regulatory developments expected in the coming months include:

- **Updated EFSA guidance** — On the safety evaluation of novel recycling technologies
- **PPWR implementation rules** — Detailed implementing acts for the Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation
- **Digital Product Passport** — Requirements for tracking material composition and recycled content through the value chain

The regulation demonstrates the EU's commitment to creating a comprehensive regulatory framework that supports the transition to a circular plastics economy while maintaining the highest standards of food safety.
